Moroccan Tagine

A slow-cooked North African masterpiece — tender meat, sweet dried fruits, and a symphony of ancient spices in a conical clay pot.

The tagine is both the name of the dish and the distinctive conical clay vessel it's cooked in. The cone-shaped lid traps steam and returns moisture to the stew, creating incredibly tender results. Moroccan tagines often combine savoury and sweet elements — lamb with apricots, chicken with preserved lemons and olives.

Ingredients
  • 800 g lamb shoulder, cut into chunks
  • 2 onions, finely sliced
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 100 g dried apricots
  • 2 preserved lemons, quartered
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp ground coriander
  • 0.5 tsp saffron threads, soaked in warm water
  • 1 tsp ras el hanout
  • 1 tsp ground ginger
  • 400 ml chicken stock
  • Fresh coriander and toasted almonds to garnish
Cooking Instructions
  1. Mix all spices together. Coat lamb pieces in the spice mix and marinate for at least 1 hour (overnight is best).
  2. Heat oil in the tagine base (or a heavy casserole). Brown the lamb in batches and set aside.
  3. In the same pot, soften onions for 8 minutes. Add garlic and cook 1 minute more.
  4. Return lamb to the pot. Add saffron water, stock, and preserved lemons.
  5. Cover and cook on very low heat for 1.5–2 hours until lamb is fall-apart tender.
  6. Add dried apricots in the last 20 minutes of cooking.
  7. Garnish with fresh coriander and toasted almonds. Serve with couscous or flatbread.
Cultural Background

Tagine cooking dates back to the Berber people of North Africa. The dish was popularised across the Maghreb region and later influenced by Arab, Moorish, and Ottoman culinary traditions. Today, Moroccan tagine is recognised by UNESCO as part of the Mediterranean diet's cultural heritage. The combination of sweet and savoury flavours reflects the ancient spice trade routes that passed through Morocco.
