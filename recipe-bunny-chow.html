Bunny Chow

Durban's most iconic street food — a hollowed-out loaf of white bread packed with rich, fragrant curry. Born in South Africa's Indian community, loved by everyone.

Bunny Chow originated in Durban among the Indian-South African community in the 1940s. Workers needed a portable, affordable meal — and the hollowed bread loaf was the perfect edible container. Today it's a national treasure, served with the scooped-out bread "virgin" on the side for dipping.

Ingredients
  • 1 unsliced white bread loaf
  • 600 g chicken thighs or lamb, cubed
  • 2 large onions, finely chopped
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, grated
  • 2 tomatoes, chopped
  • 2 tbsp Durban curry powder
  • 1 tsp turmeric
  • 1 tsp cumin seeds
  • 2 potatoes, cubed
  • 3 tbsp vegetable oil
  • Salt and fresh coriander to taste
Cooking Instructions
  1. Heat oil in a heavy pot. Fry cumin seeds for 30 seconds until fragrant.
  2. Add onions and cook on medium heat for 10 minutes until golden brown.
  3. Add garlic and ginger, cook 2 minutes. Add curry powder and turmeric, stir for 1 minute.
  4. Add tomatoes and cook until they break down into a thick paste, about 8 minutes.
  5. Add meat and coat well in the masala. Brown for 5 minutes.
  6. Add potatoes and enough water to just cover. Simmer covered for 35–40 minutes until meat is tender and sauce is thick.
  7. Cut the bread loaf into quarters (or halves for a full "quarter bunny"). Hollow out the centre, leaving a thick bread shell.
  8. Fill generously with curry. Place the scooped bread on top. Serve immediately.
Cultural Background

The name "Bunny Chow" has nothing to do with rabbits — "bunny" is believed to derive from "bania", a term for the Indian merchant caste. The dish was created out of necessity during apartheid, when Indian South Africans were barred from entering many restaurants and needed a takeaway meal that required no cutlery. Today it's a symbol of Durban's multicultural identity and Indian-South African culinary heritage.
