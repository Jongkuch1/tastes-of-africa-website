Jollof Rice

West Africa's most beloved one-pot rice dish — smoky, rich, and deeply satisfying. A staple at every celebration from Lagos to Accra.

Jollof Rice is more than a dish — it's a cultural institution. Originating from the Wolof people of Senegal, it has evolved into countless regional variations across West Africa. The smoky bottom crust (called "party jollof") is considered the ultimate prize.

Ingredients
  • 2 cups long-grain parboiled rice
  • 400 g canned crushed tomatoes
  • 2 red bell peppers, blended
  • 2 scotch bonnet peppers (adjust to taste)
  • 1 large onion, divided
  • 3 tbsp tomato paste
  • 4 tbsp vegetable oil
  • 2 cups chicken or vegetable stock
  • Salt, curry powder, thyme, bay leaves to taste
Cooking Instructions
  1. Blend tomatoes, red peppers, scotch bonnet, and half the onion into a smooth purée.
  2. Heat oil in a heavy-bottomed pot. Fry the remaining sliced onion until golden.
  3. Add tomato paste and fry for 2 minutes, then pour in the blended pepper mix.
  4. Cook the tomato base on medium heat for 20–25 minutes, stirring often, until the oil floats to the top.
  5. Season with salt, curry powder, thyme, and bay leaves. Add stock and bring to a boil.
  6. Wash rice and add to the pot. Stir, cover tightly with foil then the lid.
  7. Cook on low heat for 30 minutes. Check and stir from the bottom. Cook another 10 minutes for the smoky crust.
  8. Serve with fried plantain, coleslaw, or grilled chicken.
Cultural Background

The "Jollof Wars" between Nigeria and Ghana are a beloved cultural debate — each country claims the superior version. Nigerian Jollof is known for its smoky depth, while Ghanaian Jollof often uses basmati rice and a slightly different spice profile. Senegalese Thiéboudienne is considered the original ancestor of all Jollof dishes.
