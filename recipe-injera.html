Injera & Doro Wot

Ethiopia's ancient sourdough flatbread — the plate, the utensil, and the meal all in one. Served with rich, slow-cooked stews.

Injera is made from teff — a tiny, nutrient-dense grain native to the Ethiopian highlands. The batter ferments for 2–3 days, developing a distinctive sour flavour. It's cooked on a large flat griddle called a mitad, creating a spongy, porous surface perfect for scooping up stews.

Injera Ingredients
  • 2 cups teff flour
  • 2.5 cups water (room temperature)
  • 0.5 tsp salt
  • Starter: 2 tbsp of previous injera batter or active sourdough starter
Doro Wot (Chicken Stew) Ingredients
  • 1 kg chicken pieces
  • 4 large onions, finely chopped
  • 4 tbsp berbere spice
  • 4 tbsp niter kibbeh (spiced clarified butter)
  • 4 hard-boiled eggs, pierced
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
  • Salt to taste
Injera Cooking Instructions
  1. Mix teff flour with water and starter. Cover loosely and ferment at room temperature for 2–3 days.
  2. When ready, the batter should smell pleasantly sour and have bubbles on the surface.
  3. Heat a large non-stick pan or griddle over medium-high heat. Lightly grease.
  4. Pour a ladleful of batter in a spiral from the outside in, tilting to spread thinly.
  5. Cover and cook for 2–3 minutes until the surface is dry and full of holes. Do not flip.
  6. Remove and cool on a clean cloth. Repeat with remaining batter.
Doro Wot Cooking Instructions
  1. Dry-fry onions in a heavy pot for 15 minutes, stirring constantly, until very soft and golden.
  2. Add niter kibbeh and berbere. Cook for 5 minutes, stirring.
  3. Add garlic and ginger. Cook 2 minutes.
  4. Add chicken pieces. Coat well in the sauce. Add a little water if needed.
  5. Cover and simmer on low heat for 45 minutes until chicken is very tender.
  6. Add hard-boiled eggs in the last 10 minutes. Season with salt.
  7. Serve on injera with additional stews like misir wot (red lentils).
Cultural Background

In Ethiopian culture, sharing injera is an act of community and love. The practice of gursha — feeding a piece of injera with stew directly into another person's mouth — is a gesture of deep affection and friendship. Injera has been a staple in Ethiopia and Eritrea for over 2,000 years and is central to every meal, ceremony, and celebration.
