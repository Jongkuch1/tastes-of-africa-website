Egusi Soup

Nigeria's most celebrated soup — a thick, hearty stew made from ground melon seeds, leafy greens, and assorted meats, served with fufu or pounded yam.

Egusi soup is made from the ground seeds of egusi melon (Citrullus lanatus), which thicken the soup and give it a rich, nutty flavour. It is one of the most widely consumed soups in West and Central Africa, with each region and family having its own cherished variation.

Ingredients
  • 2 cups ground egusi (melon seeds)
  • 500 g assorted meat (beef, tripe, or goat)
  • 200 g stockfish or smoked fish
  • 4 tbsp palm oil
  • 2 cups bitter leaf or spinach, chopped
  • 2 scotch bonnet peppers, blended
  • 2 tomatoes, blended
  • 1 onion, divided
  • 2 tbsp ground crayfish
  • Stock cubes and salt to taste
Cooking Instructions
  1. Season and cook assorted meat with half the onion, stock cubes, and salt until tender. Reserve the stock.
  2. Heat palm oil in a large pot. Fry the remaining sliced onion for 3 minutes.
  3. Add blended pepper and tomato mix. Fry on medium heat for 15 minutes, stirring, until the oil rises to the top.
  4. Mix ground egusi with a little water to form a thick paste. Drop spoonfuls into the pot.
  5. Do not stir — let the egusi cook undisturbed for 5 minutes to form clumps, then gently fold in.
  6. Add cooked meat, stockfish, crayfish, and enough meat stock to reach desired consistency.
  7. Simmer for 15 minutes. Add bitter leaf or spinach in the last 5 minutes.
  8. Serve hot with fufu, pounded yam, or eba.
Cultural Background

Egusi soup holds deep cultural significance across Nigeria, Ghana, Cameroon, and beyond. In Yoruba culture it is called efo elegusi, while the Igbo call it ofe egusi. It is a dish of celebration and abundance — always present at weddings, naming ceremonies, and festivals. The combination of palm oil, crayfish, and bitter leaf creates a flavour profile that is unmistakably West African.
