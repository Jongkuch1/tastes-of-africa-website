Isombe

A beloved Rwandan dish of slow-cooked cassava leaves with eggplant, peanuts, and palm oil — earthy, rich, and deeply nourishing.

Isombe is a staple of Rwandan home cooking, made from cassava leaves — the leafy tops of the cassava plant that are often discarded elsewhere but treasured in Central Africa. The leaves are pounded or finely chopped, then slow-cooked with palm oil and groundnut paste until silky and deeply flavourful.

Ingredients
  • 500 g fresh or frozen cassava leaves, finely chopped
  • 1 medium eggplant, cubed
  • 3 tbsp palm oil
  • 4 tbsp groundnut (peanut) paste
  • 1 onion, finely chopped
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 tomatoes, chopped
  • 200 ml water or vegetable stock
  • Salt and chilli to taste
Cooking Instructions
  1. If using fresh cassava leaves, pound or blend them finely. Boil for 15 minutes to remove bitterness, then drain.
  2. Heat palm oil in a heavy pot over medium heat. Fry onion until soft, about 7 minutes.
  3. Add garlic and tomatoes. Cook until tomatoes break down, about 8 minutes.
  4. Add the cassava leaves and eggplant. Stir to combine.
  5. Stir in groundnut paste and stock. Mix well.
  6. Cover and simmer on low heat for 30–40 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the leaves are very tender and the sauce is thick.
  7. Season with salt and chilli. Serve with ugali, rice, or boiled plantain.
Cultural Background

Isombe is deeply embedded in Rwandan culture and is considered a dish of comfort and home. It is commonly served at family gatherings and celebrations. The use of cassava leaves reflects the ingenuity of Central African cooking — transforming every part of a plant into nourishment. The dish is also popular in the Democratic Republic of Congo, where it is known as pondu.
